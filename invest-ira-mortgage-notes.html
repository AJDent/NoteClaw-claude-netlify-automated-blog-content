How to Invest My Retirement Account in Real Estate

What to do with my IRA to get higher returns? Learn how to roll over your 401k to buy mortgage notes and invest your retirement account in real estate.

AJ
AJ Dent · Take Notes Capital

Can You Really Invest Your IRA in Mortgage Notes?

Yes. And people are doing it right now — earning 12–30%+ annual returns on mortgage notes, completely tax-deferred or tax-free, inside their retirement accounts.

If you've got an IRA or old 401(k) sitting in mutual funds earning 7% in a good year, this might be the most important thing you read this month.

The Problem With Traditional Retirement Accounts

Your 401(k) or IRA at Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard has a dirty little secret: they only let you invest in what they sell. Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs.

The IRS actually allows retirement accounts to invest in almost anything — real estate, private lending, mortgage notes, businesses, precious metals, and more.

What Is a Self-Directed IRA (SDIRA)?

A Self-Directed IRA is an IRA held at a custodian that allows you to invest in alternative assets — including mortgage notes.

Think about that. You buy a non-performing note for $25,000 inside a Roth SDIRA. Over the next 2 years, you collect $45,000 in payments and payoffs. That $20,000 profit is yours — tax-free. No capital gains. No income tax. Nothing.

How to Set Up an SDIRA for Note Investing

1. Choose a Custodian: Equity Trust, Entrust, IRA Financial, Directed IRA, etc.

2. Fund the Account: Roll over funds from an old 401(k) or existing IRA.

3. Buy Notes: Instruct the custodian to purchase notes on behalf of your IRA.

The Rules (Don't Break These)

No self-dealing, no disqualified persons (family), no personal use of IRA-held properties.

Why Mortgage Notes Are Perfect for SDIRAs

Predictable cash flow, no property management, lower capital requirements, and multiple exit strategies.

The Compound Effect

If you start with $50,000 and earn 20% annually through note investing, your money grows to over $1.9 million in 20 years — tax-free in a Roth.

The vehicle matters. The asset class matters more.
