Rental Properties vs Note Investing: The Honest Comparison

Same $50K. Two different paths. Here's exactly what happens in Year 1 — no guru math, no best-case scenarios, just real numbers.

AJ
AJ Dent · Take Notes Capital

I'm not going to trash rental properties. Plenty of people have built real wealth with rentals, and I respect the grind. But I'm also not going to pretend they're the only — or even the best — way to invest in real estate.

If you're comparing rental properties to mortgage note investing, you deserve the honest numbers. Not the "best case scenario" from a guru selling a course. Not the "it's totally passive" pitch from someone who's never taken a 2 AM phone call about a broken water heater.

Here's the real comparison.

The Setup: Same $50,000, Two Different Paths

To make this fair, let's say you have $50,000 to invest. You're choosing between a rental property and a portfolio of mortgage notes. Let's track what actually happens over Year 1.

Path A: Rental Property

The Buy:

  • Purchase price: $180,000
  • Down payment (20%): $36,000
  • Closing costs: $5,000
  • Initial repairs/make-ready: $8,000
  • Reserves set aside: $1,000
  • Total cash deployed: $50,000
  • Mortgage: $144,000 at 7.2% = ~$978/month

Monthly Income:

  • Rent collected: $1,500/month
  • Mortgage payment: -$978
  • Property taxes (escrowed): -$175
  • Insurance: -$100
  • Property management (10%): -$150
  • Maintenance reserve (5%): -$75
  • Vacancy reserve (8%): -$120
  • Net monthly cash flow: -$98 (yes, negative)

Wait — negative cash flow? On paper, the deal looks great. But when you account for all actual expenses, management fees, and reserves for vacancy and maintenance, many rentals in today's market cash flow negative or barely break even.

Year 1 Reality:

  • Cash flow: -$1,176 (let's call it break-even with some optimistic months)
  • Principal paydown: ~$2,400
  • Appreciation (3%): ~$5,400
  • Tax benefits: ~$2,000 (depreciation)
  • Total Year 1 return: ~$8,624 on $50K = 17.2%

Not bad on paper. But that 17.2% includes appreciation you can't spend, principal paydown you can't access, and tax benefits that vary by your situation. Your actual cash in pocket is roughly zero.

And we haven't talked about the call at 11 PM when the AC breaks ($4,000), the tenant who stops paying in month 8 (2 months to evict + $3,000 in legal fees), or the cap-ex surprise (roof needs replacing in year 3 for $12,000).

Path B: Mortgage Note Portfolio

The Buy:

  • Note 1: Non-performing 1st at $25,000 (UPB $65,000, FMV $95,000)
  • Note 2: Non-performing 2nd at $8,000 (UPB $28,000, FMV $140,000)
  • Note 3: Non-performing 2nd at $6,000 (UPB $22,000, FMV $110,000)
  • Due diligence costs: ~$3,000
  • Servicer setup: $500
  • Reserves: $7,500
  • Total cash deployed: $50,000

Year 1 Outcomes:

  • Note 1: Loan mod completed month 3. Borrower paying $425/month. 9 months of payments = $3,825. Note now re-performing — could sell for ~$48,000.
  • Note 2: DPO negotiated month 5. Borrower settled for $14,000. Profit: $6,000 in 5 months.
  • Note 3: Modification attempted, borrower unresponsive. Sold the note at month 8 for $9,500. Profit: $3,500.

Year 1 Reality:

  • Cash received: $3,825 (payments) + $14,000 (DPO) + $9,500 (note sale) = $27,325
  • Minus costs: $50,000 invested
  • Note 1 still held (performing asset worth ~$48,000)
  • Portfolio value + cash: $75,325
  • Year 1 return: ~50.7% on $50K

No tenants. No maintenance calls. No property taxes. No insurance headaches. Three deals instead of one — if one goes sideways, the others carry.

Rentals build wealth slowly through appreciation and principal paydown. Notes build wealth through actual cash returns. Both work — but if you need the money to work now, notes have the edge.

Hassle Factor

Rental property — what you're signing up for:

  • Finding and screening tenants
  • Handling maintenance requests
  • Dealing with property management (or doing it yourself)
  • Eviction proceedings when tenants don't pay
  • Insurance claims, property inspections, code violations
  • Being a landlord (whether you call yourself one or not)

Note investing — what you're signing up for:

  • Reviewing loan tapes and doing due diligence
  • Working with a servicer (they handle borrower contact)
  • Making strategic decisions (modify, DPO, foreclose)
  • Monitoring your portfolio
  • No properties to manage. No tenants. No physical assets to maintain.

I won't pretend note investing is zero effort. There's a learning curve, and active deal management takes time. But you're making financial decisions, not plumbing decisions.

Risk Profile

Rental risks:

  • Tenant damage / non-payment / eviction
  • Market downturn reduces property value and rent potential
  • Cap-ex surprises (HVAC, roof, foundation)
  • Regulatory risk (rent control, eviction moratoriums)
  • Concentration risk — one property, one market, one tenant
  • Liability risk — someone slips on your property, you get sued

Note investing risks:

  • Borrower doesn't respond (longer timeline to resolution)
  • Property value declines below your basis
  • Foreclosure costs and timelines in judicial states
  • Regulatory changes to lending/servicing rules
  • 2nd position notes can be wiped out if the 1st forecloses

Both have risk. The difference is diversification. $50,000 buys you ONE rental. $50,000 buys you 3–5 notes across different states, borrowers, and exit strategies. One bad outcome doesn't sink your portfolio.

Scalability

Rentals: Every new property requires a new down payment, new financing, new insurance, new management. At some point, banks stop lending to you (debt-to-income limits). Scaling requires increasingly creative financing.

Notes: Every new note requires capital and due diligence. That's it. No loan qualification. No inspections. No insurance requirements. You can buy notes in any state from your laptop. Scale with your own capital, SDIRAs, private investors, or JV partners.

Tax Treatment

Rentals:

  • Depreciation deductions (major benefit)
  • Mortgage interest deduction
  • 1031 exchange on sale (defer capital gains)
  • Passive income rules can limit deductibility

Notes:

  • Interest income (ordinary income tax rates)
  • Short-term capital gains on quick flips
  • Long-term capital gains if held 12+ months
  • Can be held inside SDIRAs for tax-free/tax-deferred growth
  • No depreciation benefit (you don't own property)

Rentals win on tax efficiency through depreciation. Notes win when held in retirement accounts (all growth tax-free in a Roth SDIRA).

Capital Requirements

RequirementRental PropertyNote Investing
Minimum entry$30,000–$60,000$3,000–$25,000
Financing availableYes (mortgage)No (cash only)
LeverageYes (5:1 typical)No
Additional costsTaxes, insurance, maintenanceServicer fees ($25–$50/mo)

Rentals allow leverage, which amplifies both returns and losses. Notes require cash but have far lower operating costs and no ongoing liabilities.

When Rentals Make More Sense

I'm not anti-rental. Here's when rentals are the better play:

  • You want maximum leverage — $50K controls a $250K asset with a mortgage. Notes don't offer that.
  • You want depreciation for tax benefits against active income.
  • You're in a high-appreciation market and betting on property value growth.
  • You want a tangible asset you can see, touch, and improve with sweat equity.
  • You already have infrastructure — property management, contractors, systems.

When Note Investing Makes More Sense

  • You're tired of tenant headaches and want real estate returns without property management.
  • You have limited capital and want diversification across multiple assets.
  • You have retirement funds (SDIRA/401k) you want to actively invest. See how to invest your IRA in mortgage notes.
  • You want cash flow NOW, not 10 years from now when the mortgage is paid off.
  • You value flexibility — 14 exit strategies vs. rent or sell.
  • You want geographic diversification — buy notes in any state.
  • You want to scale without banks — no loan qualification needed.

The Real Answer: They're Not Mutually Exclusive

The smartest real estate investors use both strategies. Rentals for long-term wealth building and tax benefits. Notes for cash flow, diversification, and capital recycling.

But if you've only ever considered rentals because that's all anyone talks about? You owe it to yourself to learn what the other side looks like.

No tenants. No toilets. No calls at 2 AM. Just math, strategy, and monthly cash flow.
